1.4 - Describe the risks that might impact digital projects:


Digital projects can fail due to a variety of things, from self inflicted human errors, uncontrolled failures to security breaches. I am going to elaborate on a list of reasons as to why digital projects could face potential risks of which could hold negative impact.


System malfunctions:

When creating online projects, connecting to the internet is crucial in order to research, create and publish your project. With that being said - if during the construction period of your project, the online systems were to malfunction, the whole entire will be delayed and the work you have made could potentially be set back to the quality of the last online connected value. You will be unable to access the internet for research, unavailable access to open the tools and programs required in order to create your project or the inability to save or publish it.


Security breaches:

In the event of a security breach - the entire project can come to a stand still. Many ways, a project can fall victim of an unethical hack, however, for example purposes, I am going to mention a brute force password attack. When creating a project, you and your team would have administrator rights. Therefore, if your project was to fall victim of a malicious hack, a hacker could potentially steal admin rights and corrupt the whole project.


Lack of knowledge & skills:

When creating a project independently or in a team, knowledge is a major priority and necessity as individuals may not possess the required skills that are mandatory for the process and completion of the project. If there is a deadline of completion, this will be a major concern as very little work would have been put in to the project itself.


Human errors & Malware:

Human errors can tie into many things and happens by everybody on a day to day basis. However, human errors in IT is extraordinary, the reason for this is that there is so much to learn in terms of online technology and nobody possesses the expertise of everything computers have to offer. One error could potentially crash the system and the project can fail. This may happen in numerous ways; malware is commonly the brunt of all errors. Individuals can fall victim of clicking on a malicious website link or application with the thoughts of it leading them to genuine and legitimate online resources, where in fact, could lead them to different types of malware that will have many negative effects to not only the project, but the device itself. A way to prevent this is for organisations to provide online training courses and powerpoint presentations explaining the potential threats of malware and any other negative outcomes based upon human error.

1.7 - Describe legal guidelines and constraints that impact digital projects:


GDPR stands for “General Data Protection Regulations” and is an EU guideline that came into force on May 25th 2018 regarding data protection It defines data as; “any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person including identifiers such as a name, an identification number, location data, online identifiers, or, for example, factors specific to the physical, physiological, genetic, mental, economic, cultural or social identity of that natural person”. GDPR is a new set of rules designed to give EU citizens more control over their personal data. It aims to simplify the regulatory environment for business so both citizens and businesses in the European Union can fully benefit from the digital economy. GDPR enforces that all digital companies must process personal data in the mind of minimising data, restricting the used information to the bare minimum to stop the movement flow of personal data. This helps reduce the risks impact of leaked or lost data, including collections of unnecessary information such as the usage of email addresses for future marketing purposes. Removing personal identifiers, IPs, names, addresses, credentials etc is a common way to hide data, even while collecting data such as date of birth, income and ethnic origin etc. This also makes it non-requiring with GDPR. The legislation also helps bring foreign competitors operating within the EU (which, in today's modern digital market, means globally) must also comply to EU GDPR. [4]
